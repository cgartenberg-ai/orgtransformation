# Locus of Innovation: Full Development

## Principle 4 in the AI Transformation Architecture

---

## 1. The Core Question

**Where do you expect the best ideas for AI transformation to originate, and how do you design the organizational mechanisms to cultivate, capture, and deploy them?**

### Why This Is the Anchor Principle

The "lab" question—broadly defined—is where transformation becomes concrete. It forces you to commit:
- What do you actually believe about where innovation comes from?
- What structure will you build to make it happen?
- How will it connect leadership direction to ground-level energy?
- What resources will you commit, and how will you protect them?

Every other principle ultimately manifests through this one. Your stance on uncertainty shapes how you design the lab's planning cycles. Your measurement philosophy determines how you evaluate the lab's success. Your information architecture determines how insights flow to and from the lab. Your ambidexterity structure determines how the lab relates to operations.

Get the lab wrong, and leadership direction never connects to ground-level experimentation. Get it right, and you've built the engine for continuous transformation.

## 2. The Library: Real Lab Models

These are actual organizational structures operating in the world today. Each represents a different bet on where innovation comes from and how to cultivate it.

### 2.1 Eli Lilly: Decentralized Domain Hubs

**What it is:**
Approximately 15 years ago, Lilly decentralized its R&D into smaller hubs of 300-400 people. Each hub operates like a biotech—focused on a specific therapeutic area or modality—but without the burden of fundraising or quarterly investor pressure.

**How it works:**
- Small enough to maintain scientific intimacy and shared tacit knowledge
- Large enough to have critical mass of capability
- Protected from corporate optimization pressure
- Long time horizons (the GLP-1 program ran 18+ years from first injection to Mounjaro)
- "Teams just grinding on a theme" rather than big-bet single projects

**The core insight:**
CEO Dave Ricks: "All the studies on middle management find they tend to squash deviations, but the deviations are the people doing stuff off strategy, in the labs, the things that make the next breakthrough."

**Recent AI evolution:**
In January 2026, Lilly announced a $1B co-innovation lab with NVIDIA in South San Francisco. This adds a "scientist-in-the-loop" AI capability to the existing decentralized structure—Lilly domain experts co-located with NVIDIA AI engineers. They're also deploying a 1,016 Blackwell Ultra GPU supercomputer (9+ exaflops)—the most powerful owned by a pharma company.

**Key metrics:**
- Drug development cycle: ~7 years vs. industry average of 10
- R&D investment target: 20-25% of sales
- At $120B revenue, R&D budget approaches NIH scale

**Source:** Cheeky Pint Podcast (Collison Brothers), Chief Executive Magazine, NVIDIA/Lilly press releases January 2026

### 2.2 Google X: The Moonshot Factory

**What it is:**
Alphabet's "moonshot factory"—a dedicated organization for pursuing radical, high-risk innovations with potential for 10x impact. Operates on deliberately different rules than the rest of Google.

**How it works:**
- Launches 100+ experiments annually
- 2% graduation rate (projects that become real businesses after 5-6 years)
- Explicit incentives for killing projects: "Teams kill their ideas as soon as the evidence is on the table because they're rewarded for it. They get applause from their peers. Hugs and high fives from their manager. They get promoted for it."
- "Pre-mortem" analysis before projects start (predict why it will fail)
- "Run at all the hardest parts of the problem first. Get excited and cheer, Hey! How are we going to kill our project today?"
- Surviving projects get concentrated resources: 44% of X's entire budget goes to the 2% that graduate

**The core insight:**
CEO Astro Teller: The incentive structure is the innovation. By rewarding failure and making project termination a badge of honor, X eliminates the sunk cost fallacy and political protection of zombie projects.

**Graduates:**
Waymo, Verily, Wing, Loon, Chronicle

**Source:** TechCrunch Disrupt 2025, HBR Podcast, NPR Interview

### 2.3 Anthropic: Labs Team + Ride the Exponential

**What it is:**
A two-part innovation model: (1) a dedicated **Labs team** focused on "disruptive zero-to-one ideas" that operates independently from core product, and (2) a company-wide product philosophy of "ride the exponential"—building for capabilities that don't exist yet.

**How it works:**
- **Labs team** is separate from main product organization, focused on exploratory/disruptive innovation
- Labs built Claude CLI (later Claude Code), early computer use explorations, and other experimental bets
- Once Labs creates something promising, it gets "buttoned up and put on a more fancy suit" for public release
- Internal adoption validates before external release: Claude CLI "rapidly overtook every other coding tool we had internally" between September and December before being released as Claude Code
- Regular hackathons (2-3x/year) where internal teams build on Labs innovations, discovering unexpected use cases (bioinformatics, SRE-in-a-box, data science, project management)
- Product principle: "ride the exponential"—build products that "meet the moment" but "can naturally improve" as models get better
- Over time, they **delete parts of the harness** rather than add to it because the model can do more

**The core insight:**
CPO Mike Krieger: "The more you can align with the company's general long-term perspective about where powerful AI will come from, the smoother things will go because [Anthropic] is nothing but focused."

The Labs team explores at the frontier. When they find something that works internally, it becomes product. The key is building products that get better as models improve, not building for current capabilities.

**The "ride the exponential" philosophy:**
- Build for where capabilities are going, not where they are
- Accept that current implementations will break
- Design products that naturally improve as models improve
- "We've deleted parts of the harness over time rather than added to it because the model can do more"
- Companies that "pushed the limits with earlier models, hit walls, and then were ready when new capabilities emerged" are the ones succeeding

**Internal validation as innovation filter:**
- Labs builds exploratory tools
- Internal adoption is the first test (Claude CLI overtaking other internal tools in 3 months)
- Hackathons surface unexpected use cases—every hackathon "has been around the time that some technology is poised for a breakout"
- External release follows internal proof

**Key metrics:**
- Claude Code: $1B ARR within 6 months of launch
- MCP: "fastest-growing standard in tech history"
- Internal hackathons consistently discover breakthrough applications before external market

**Source:** AI Daily Brief podcast interview with Mike Krieger (December 2025), Lenny's Newsletter, Sequoia Inference Podcast

### 2.4 Samsung C-Lab: Separation with Spinoff Path

**What it is:**
An internal incubator launched in 2012 after Samsung management's Silicon Valley experiences. Operates on two structural innovations: internal holacracy and a defined spinoff pathway.

**How it works:**
- Employees vote on ideas, experts review finalists, selected audience plays "venture capitalists" deciding whether to invest
- Selected employees get a full year away from regular duties—100% time, not 20% time
- Flat structure: "Each team has a leader and project members. That's it." No hierarchical systems.
- Teams can recruit from outside Samsung if needed talent isn't available internally
- Clear exit mechanism: 20% of C-Lab projects have become independent companies
- Since 2015, promising projects can spin off as fully-fledged startups

**The core insight:**
Corporate innovation needs both structural freedom (flat hierarchy) AND clear exit paths (spinoff mechanism) to attract entrepreneurial talent and maintain startup-like urgency.

**Scale:**
As of February 2025: 959 total ventures supported (423 in-house, 536 external through C-Lab Outside)

**Source:** Samsung Global Newsroom, MDPI Sustainability Journal

### 2.5 Tesla: Fleet as Distributed Lab

**What it is:**
Using deployed products as the innovation system. 1.5 million cars globally, each with 8 cameras, continuously generating training data and surfacing edge cases that no human R&D team could anticipate.

**How it works:**
- Former AI Director Andrej Karpathy: "Build a dataset; train your network; deploy it and test it. When you notice that the network is misbehaving, you incorporate the errors into the training set."
- Every week, robots process 1.5 petabytes of data requiring massive compute to train
- The fleet identifies edge cases that humans would never anticipate
- Vision-only approach (no LiDAR, no HD maps): "It's actually quite unscalable to collect, build, and maintain high-definition lidar maps."

**The core insight:**
Design products that generate training data as a byproduct of usage. The more customers use the product, the better it gets. This creates a flywheel competitors without deployed fleets cannot replicate.

**Cross-domain transfer:**
The organizational capability built for Autopilot directly enables Tesla Bot (Optimus). Musk: they want to "use all the same tools we use in the car" to build the robot—same neural networks, same training infrastructure, same data engine philosophy.

**Source:** Lex Fridman Podcast #333, Tesla AI Day, BrainCreators

### 2.6 Bank of America: Broad Deployment, Consumer-Grade UX

**What it is:**
Rather than a separate innovation lab, BofA's approach treats enterprise AI deployment like a consumer product problem. The "lab" is essentially a product organization focused on getting tools into everyone's hands with minimal friction.

**How it works:**
- Erica (customer-facing virtual assistant) launched 2018
- Rather than building a separate employee system, extended Erica internally in 2020 as "Erica for Employees"
- Leveraged existing brand recognition, user familiarity, and proven conversational patterns
- Pandemic timing crucial: rapid adoption when employees needed technology support for remote work
- Started with IT support, expanded to health benefits, payroll, tax forms—each expansion built on proven interaction patterns

**The core insight:**
Don't build separate enterprise and consumer AI—extend consumer-proven interfaces to employees. The best enterprise AI strategy may be "Erica for Everyone." Consumer-grade UX drives adoption; adoption drives value.

**Key metrics:**
- Over 90% of 213,000 employees actively use AI tools
- IT service desk calls reduced by more than 50%
- Developer efficiency increased 20% through coding assistants
- Over 15 years: headcount reduced from 300,000 to 212,000 while deposits doubled—$6 billion in expense savings

**Source:** BofA Newsroom, CTO Magazine, AIX Expert Network

### 2.8 McKinsey QuantumBlack: Domain Expertise Acquisition

**What it is:**
A model where AI capability was acquired through purchasing a company with deep domain expertise in a specific, high-stakes field (Formula 1 racing), then generalized to enterprise contexts.

**How it works:**
- QuantumBlack founded 2009 in London, specialized in F1 racing analytics—optimizing split-second decisions with advanced data science

**The core insight:**
The best enterprise AI capabilities may come from acquiring teams who've solved extreme versions of common problems. F1 racing analytics to supply chain optimization is a more credible path than pure consulting to AI services.

**Scale:**
From 45 people in 2015 to 1,200+ practitioners by 2022, now McKinsey's center of excellence for AI, gen AI, and agentic AI.

**Products:**

### 2.9 Recursion Pharmaceuticals: Automated Hypothesis-Free Discovery

**What it is:**
A lab where the primary "researcher" is an automated system running 2.2 million experiments per week. Humans design the system and interpret results, but the discovery process itself is largely automated and hypothesis-agnostic.

**How it works:**
- Robots run up to 2.2 million experiments weekly, generating 40 petabytes of data from over 300+ million experiments
- CEO Chris Gibson: "I'm currently sitting 100 feet away from a giant lab full of robots."
- Traditional pharma starts with hypotheses about disease mechanisms. Recursion inverts this: "The company's platform is capable of finding novel biology that may have been overlooked by humans, or considered too challenging to pursue."
- LOWE: an LLM-based interface where scientists can query across animal models, commercially available compounds, and existing research using natural language

**The core insight:**
Remove human intuition as a bottleneck by running experiments at scales no human team could achieve. Let the data reveal biology that human hypotheses would miss. The goal is a "self-driving lab."

**Efficiency claims:**
"By leveraging data, automation, and virtual modelling, Recursion develops a lead molecule with 10 times the efficiency of traditional methods by significantly reducing the number of molecules that need to be physically synthesised, in half the time and at a fraction of the cost."

**Vision:**
Building toward a "virtual cell"—and eventually a virtual organ and virtual patient—that can simulate drug effects computationally before any physical testing.

**Source:** BioPharma Dive, Fortune, NIH Seed, Recursion Press

### 2.10 P&G ChatPG: Large-Scale Field Experiments

**What it is:**
Rather than a traditional "innovation lab," P&G's approach was to build an internal AI tool and immediately deploy it to thousands of users, treating the entire company as the experimental platform.

**How it works:**
- Built ChatPG (internal generative AI tool) and rejected "small pilot then scale" approach
- Decision: test with thousands of people immediately to get it in use quickly
- "In the first two hours when they launched at P&G, they received 8,000 requests"
- Company-wide launch followed within 2-3 months
- Partnership with Harvard Business School's Digital Data Design Institute for rigorous academic validation
- Field experiment with 776 professionals: "Individuals using chatPG achieved the same performance level as teams that did not use the AI tool, while teams that partnered with chatPG consistently produced the best outcomes"

**The core insight:**
For enterprise AI adoption, skip small pilots. Deploy broadly, measure rigorously with academic partners, and iterate fast based on real usage data rather than theoretical concerns.

**Architecture:**
Platform enables switching between AI models "as deemed necessary from a business perspective"—avoiding vendor lock-in while maintaining security ("walled garden")

**Scale:**
30,000+ users, mandatory 10-minute training and acceptable use policy, 35+ use cases with internal data integration. Three deployed products: ChatPG, ImagePG, AskPG.

**Source:** CIO Dive, MIT Sloan Management Review, P&G Signal

## 3. The Design Principles

These are the underlying insights extracted from studying the labs above. They're not mutually exclusive—a good lab design might combine several. And they're not exhaustive—new principles will emerge as we learn more.

---

### Principle A: Protect Deviations from Optimization Pressure

**The insight:** Middle management systematically kills off-strategy work because their job is to optimize current operations. Breakthrough innovation requires structural protection from this pressure.

**Manifestations:**
- Separate organizational units with different reporting lines (Eli Lilly hubs, Google X)
- Ring-fenced budgets that require board approval to reallocate
- Leaders who explicitly defend "unproductive" work
- Physical separation (different buildings, different cities)
- Different time horizons (annual reviews vs. quarterly)

**The test:** When the core business is under pressure, what happens to exploration resources? If they get raided, protection is insufficient.

---

### Principle B: Reward Fast Failure Explicitly

**The insight:** Most organizations implicitly punish failure even when they say they value experimentation. Explicit rewards for killing projects early (bonuses, promotions, public celebration) are required to overcome this.

**Manifestations:**
- Google X: "We have bonused every single person on teams that end their projects"
- Pre-mortems before starting (predict why this will fail)
- "Run at the hardest parts first" methodology
- Public celebration of killed projects, not just successes
- Promotion criteria that include "projects responsibly ended"

**The test:** Can you name the last project that was killed early and what happened to the people who killed it? If they were penalized or sidelined, the incentives are wrong.

---

### Principle C: Ride the Exponential (Build for Future Capabilities)

**The insight:** If you build for what AI can reliably do today, you'll be behind when capabilities improve (which happens fast). Build for what's barely possible, accept breakage, and be ready when capabilities arrive. Design products that naturally get better as models improve—and delete scaffolding over time rather than adding to it.

**Manifestations:**
- Anthropic product principle: "ride the exponential"—products must "meet the moment" but "naturally improve" as models get better
- Deleting harness code over time rather than adding to it because the model can do more
- Companies that "pushed the limits with earlier models, hit walls, and then were ready when new capabilities emerged" are the ones succeeding (Cursor, Lovable)
- Designing for 10x capability improvement in 18 months
- Accepting that current implementations will break

**The test:** Is your AI roadmap based on current model capabilities or anticipated capabilities? Are you deleting scaffolding over time or adding more? If you're only building for what works reliably today, you're too conservative.

---

### Principle D: Internal-First Validation Before External Release

**The insight:** The best filter for whether an innovation is real is whether your own organization adopts it. Internal usage surfaces problems, discovers unexpected applications, and builds conviction before external release.

**Manifestations:**
- Anthropic: Labs builds tools, internal adoption validates (Claude CLI overtook other tools in 3 months), then external release
- Hackathons as discovery mechanism—internal teams find use cases the builders didn't anticipate
- "Dogfooding" as serious innovation filter, not just quality check
- Time between internal success and external release used to "button it up and put on a fancy suit"

**The test:** How long do you use your own innovations before releasing them? If internal teams aren't fighting over access, it might not be ready.

---

### Principle E: Create Exit Paths for Entrepreneurial Energy

**The insight:** The most entrepreneurial people will leave if they feel trapped. Creating legitimate paths to "exit" (spinoffs, internal ventures, equity participation) keeps them inside the system.

**Manifestations:**
- Samsung C-Lab: 20% of projects become independent companies
- Corporate venture structures with equity participation
- Clear criteria for when a project "graduates" out of the lab
- Alumni networks that maintain connection even after spinoff

**The test:** Do your most entrepreneurial people see a path to meaningful upside? If the only way to get startup-like returns is to leave, they will.

---

### Principle G: Use Deployed Products as Data Flywheel

**The insight:** Products in the field generate data that improves AI capabilities that improve products. This flywheel is a durable competitive advantage.

**Manifestations:**
- Tesla: 1.5 million cars generating training data continuously
- Duolingo: 1.25 billion daily exercises training personalization algorithms
- Any product where usage improves the underlying AI

**The test:** Is your AI getting better as a direct result of customer usage? If not, you're not building a flywheel.

---

### Principle H: Consumer-Grade UX Drives Enterprise Adoption

**The insight:** Enterprise tools don't have to feel like enterprise tools. Consumer-grade UX dramatically increases adoption, and adoption is where value comes from.

**Manifestations:**
- Bank of America: extending consumer Erica to employees, achieving 90%+ adoption
- "Erica for Everyone" philosophy
- Measuring adoption like a consumer product (DAU, retention, NPS)

**The test:** Would your employees choose to use your internal AI tools if they weren't required? If not, the UX isn't good enough.

---

### Principle I: Rapid Iteration Cycles Over Perfect Launches

**The insight:** In fast-moving domains, speed of learning beats quality of initial launch. 8-week cycles mean you're never far from course correction.

**Manifestations:**
- JPMorgan: 8-week update cycles for LLM Suite
- DNP: 90% of use cases showing results in 90 days
- Continuous deployment over big-bang releases

**The test:** How long between identifying a problem and deploying a fix? If it's months, you're too slow.

---

### Principle J: Mandatory Proficiency Creates Universal Capability

**The insight:** Optional AI adoption creates a bimodal distribution (power users and non-users). Mandatory proficiency with support structures creates universal capability.

**Manifestations:**
- Moderna: 100% AI proficiency goal within 6 months
- Shopify: "prove AI can't do it before requesting headcount"
- AI proficiency in performance reviews
- Structured AI Champions/advocate programs

**The test:** What percentage of employees use AI tools weekly? If it's under 50%, adoption is optional in practice regardless of what policy says.

---

### Principle K: Domain Expertise is the Differentiator

**The insight:** General AI capabilities are increasingly commoditized. Domain expertise in applying AI to specific contexts is the source of differentiation.

**Manifestations:**
- McKinsey acquiring QuantumBlack (F1 racing expertise)
- Eli Lilly's 18-year GLP-1 journey (protein engineering expertise)
- Recursion's 300M+ experiments (biology-specific automation expertise)

**The test:** What do you know about applying AI to your domain that a general AI consultancy doesn't? If nothing, you're not building durable advantage.

---

### Principle L: Remove Human Intuition Bottlenecks at Scale

**The insight:** In some domains, human intuition about what to try is the bottleneck. Hypothesis-free exploration at massive scale finds what humans would miss.

**Manifestations:**
- Recursion: 2.2M experiments/week, hypothesis-agnostic discovery
- Letting data reveal patterns rather than testing human hunches
- "The company's platform is capable of finding novel biology that may have been overlooked by humans"

**The test:** Are your most valuable discoveries ones that humans predicted, or ones that surprised them? If everything was predicted, you might not be exploring broadly enough.

---

### Principle M: Information Symmetry Enables Distributed Innovation

**The insight:** When everyone has complete context, you don't need a separate innovation function—innovation can happen anywhere. Hierarchy creates information asymmetry that kills distributed innovation.

**Manifestations:**
- NVIDIA: 60 direct reports, no 1:1s, public reasoning
- Daily "Top 5" emails from across the company
- Decisions made in group settings with full context

**The test:** Does a frontline employee have the context to recognize and act on an innovation opportunity? If not, innovation is bottlenecked at the top.

---

### Principle N: Protected Exploration Time (Not Unprotected 20% Time)

**The insight:** Unprotected "20% time" fails because operational demands always win when there's no structural protection. But full separation isn't the only answer—what matters is that exploration time is *protected*, not that it's 100%.

**The spectrum of models:**

1. **Full separation (Samsung C-Lab, Google X):** 100% dedicated for extended periods (6-12 months). Strongest protection, but requires scale to absorb talent loss. Best for deep, long-horizon exploration.

2. **Dedicated core + rotating contributors:** Small full-time nucleus (5-10 people) maintains continuity; operational people do 3-6 month "tours of duty" then return. Rotators bring fresh problems and carry insights back.

3. **Time-boxed sprints (tiger teams):** Pull people out fully but for shorter bursts (6-8 weeks) to attack specific problems, then return. Works for applied innovation; less effective for fundamental exploration.

4. **Protected time blocks with structural commitment:** Not unprotected 20% time, but something like "Fridays are lab days" with explicit executive commitment that this time is sacred—meaning no one schedules operational meetings, no one assigns operational work. Requires cultural enforcement.

5. **Parallel staffing:** Hire new people specifically for the lab rather than pulling from operations. Avoids talent drain but may lack organizational context. Consider pairing with internal "translators."

**The failure mode:** The common failure is declaring "20% time" or "innovation hours" without any structural protection. When deadlines hit, that time evaporates. If you can't protect the time, don't pretend you have it.

**The test:** When was the last time exploration time survived contact with an operational crisis? If it always gets sacrificed, protection is insufficient—regardless of what percentage you've declared.

---

### Principle O: Ring-Fence the Budget (Board-Level Protection)

**The insight:** Lab budgets get raided when the core business is under pressure—unless they're structurally protected. The protection needs to be at a level above the people who feel quarterly pressure.

**Manifestations:**
- Ring-fenced budgets that require board vote (not just CEO discretion) to reallocate
- Google X: Survives because Alphabet structure protects it from Google's P&L pressure
- Eli Lilly: CEO explicitly defends "unproductive" R&D from middle management optimization
- The failure mode: Lab budget is "discretionary" and gets cut first in any downturn

**Budget structure options:**
- Percentage of revenue automatically allocated (like R&D commitments)
- Multi-year commitments that can't be unwound annually
- Separate P&L that reports to board, not operating executives
- Endowment model: principal protected, only spend returns

**The test:** When your core business had its last bad quarter, what happened to exploration budgets? If they got cut, protection is insufficient.

---

### Principle P: Governance That Lets It Cook (Without Spinning Wheels)

**The insight:** Too much oversight kills exploration; too little enables drift and waste. The governance model must create accountability for learning and progress without demanding premature proof of ROI.

**Manifestations:**
- Google X: "Pre-mortems" before projects start (predict why this will fail), then "run at the hardest parts first"—governance focused on quality of exploration, not results
- JPMorgan: 8-week cycles create natural rhythm for review without micromanagement
- Samsung C-Lab: Employees vote on ideas, experts review finalists—peer governance, not executive oversight
- The failure mode: Quarterly business reviews that demand ROI projections for exploratory work

**Governance design questions:**
- What's the review cadence? (Too frequent = micromanagement; too infrequent = drift)
- What are you reviewing—outputs or quality of exploration?
- Who reviews—peers who understand exploration, or executives who want ROI?
- What triggers escalation vs. what triggers termination?

**The test:** Can a lab team explain what they learned last quarter without being asked what they shipped? Is "we learned this won't work and killed it" a successful outcome in your governance model?

---

### Principle Q: A-Team Capability (However You Get It)

**The insight:** Labs staffed with "whoever is available" produce mediocre results. You need A-team capability in the lab—but there are multiple ways to get it beyond pulling your best operational people.

**The spectrum of models:**

1. **Pull and backfill (premium model):** Move top performers to the lab, explicitly hire replacements for their operational roles. Cleanest but most expensive. Works when you can afford it and when operational knowledge is critical.

2. **Hire externally for the lab:** Recruit specifically for exploration rather than depleting operations. Brings fresh thinking and external perspective. Risk: may lack organizational context. Mitigation: pair external hires with internal "translators" who know the organization.

3. **Hybrid teams:** Mix of dedicated external hires (for capability) and internal rotators (for context and political navigation). The externals do the technical work; the internals help it survive contact with the organization.

4. **Lab as talent magnet:** Use the lab to attract people you couldn't otherwise recruit. The exploration work is the draw—you're adding capability, not depleting operations. Works especially well for technical talent who want to work on hard problems.

5. **Fractional/advisory model:** For organizations that truly can't dedicate people, bring in external expertise on a fractional basis (advisors, consultants, academics) to guide and upskill internal part-timers. Weaker than dedicated teams but more feasible for smaller orgs.

6. **Rotation with return path:** Operational people do 6-12 month lab rotations with guaranteed return to operations (often at a higher level). Attracts ambitious people who see it as a growth opportunity, not a career detour.

**What doesn't work:**
- Asking people to "also" do innovation on top of their full operational load
- Staffing the lab with people who couldn't make it in operations
- Assuming that enthusiasm substitutes for capability

**Design questions:**
- How do you identify who should staff the lab? (Volunteers? Nominations? External recruitment?)
- If pulling internal people, what happens to their roles? (Backfilled? Distributed? Eliminated?)
- How long is the commitment? (Fixed term with return path? Permanent? Project-based?)
- How do you handle resentment from operational teams who lose talent or budget?

**The test:** Would your lab team be competitive in the external talent market for their roles? If not, you don't have A-team capability—you have who was available.

---

### Principle R: Design the Lab-to-Operations Handoff (Or It Dies in Translation)

**The insight:** Discoveries that stay in the lab create no value. But the handoff to operations is where most innovations die—either rejected by "not invented here" or mangled in translation. The handoff mechanism must be designed, not assumed.

**Manifestations:**
- Anthropic: Internal adoption validates before external release—the organization is the first customer
- Google X: 2% graduation rate, but graduates (Waymo, Wing) get 44% of budget—massive investment in successful handoffs
- JPMorgan: ML Center of Excellence deploys to 250,000 employees with 8-week cycles—tight feedback loop
- Bank of America: Extended consumer product (Erica) to employees—familiar interface eases adoption
- The failure mode: Lab builds something great, throws it over the wall, operations ignores it

**Handoff design questions:**
- Who owns the translation—lab pushes or operations pulls?
- What's the pilot structure before broad deployment?
- How do you prevent "not invented here" rejection?
- What support does the lab provide post-handoff?
- How does feedback flow back to the lab?

**The test:** Can you trace the last three lab innovations to actual operational deployment? If they're still "in pilot" or "being evaluated," your handoff is broken.

---

### Principle S: CEO as Political Shield (Not Just Sponsor)

**The insight:** Labs attract organizational antibodies—resentment from those who lost talent, skepticism from those who want ROI, competition from those who want the resources. The CEO must actively defend the lab, not just approve it.

**Manifestations:**
- Eli Lilly CEO Dave Ricks explicitly defends deviations: "All the studies on middle management find they tend to squash deviations, but the deviations are the people doing stuff off strategy, in the labs, the things that make the next breakthrough"
- NVIDIA: Jensen Huang's "mission as the boss" philosophy protects unconventional approaches
- The failure mode: CEO approves lab in year 1, is silent when attacks come in year 2, lab gets gutted by year 3

**What active defense looks like:**
- CEO publicly credits lab for value (even when attribution is ambiguous)
- CEO defends lab budget in board meetings when pressure to cut
- CEO promotes lab successes in earnings calls and investor communications
- CEO intervenes when operational leaders try to raid lab talent
- CEO asks about lab in the same forums where operational results are reviewed

**The test:** When was the last time the CEO publicly defended the lab against internal criticism? If you can't remember, the lab is politically exposed.

## 4. The Diagnostic: Building a Picture of the Focal Organization

### 4.1 Pre-Conversation Intelligence Gathering

Before any diagnostic conversation, the tool should build a baseline understanding of the organization from public sources:

**Industry and Competitive Context:**
- What industry, and what are the specific AI dynamics in that industry?
- What are peers doing? (Reference specific examples from the library)
- What's the regulatory environment?
- What's the talent market for AI in their geography/industry?

**Organizational Structure:**
- How is the company organized? (Divisions, business units, therapeutic areas, geographies)
- Where does technology currently report? (CIO, CTO, CDO, directly to CEO?)
- What's the recent history of reorgs?

**Public AI Signals:**
- What have executives said about AI in earnings calls, interviews, press releases?
- What AI partnerships or investments have been announced?
- What AI products or capabilities have been launched?
- Have there been any AI-related leadership changes (CAIO appointments, etc.)?

**Strategic Context:**
- What's the current strategic narrative? (Growth, transformation, efficiency, turnaround?)
- What are the obvious pressures? (Patent cliffs, competitive threats, regulatory changes, activist investors?)
- What's the board composition and likely AI sophistication?

---

### 4.2 Opening Context Questions

These establish the immediate situation—not diagnostic yet, but necessary framing:

**What triggered this conversation now?**
- Competitive pressure (specific competitor moves)
- Board mandate or investor pressure
- New leadership (CEO, CAIO, CTO)
- Failed previous initiative
- Specific capability gap revealed
- Strategic planning cycle
- External catalyst (regulation, market shift)

**Where does the AI conversation currently sit organizationally?**
- CEO directly driving
- CDO/CTO/CIO owned
- Chief AI Officer (and where do they report?)
- Strategy function
- Distributed across business units
- Unclear/contested

**Who's in the room for transformation decisions?**
- CEO alone
- Full C-suite
- Specific subset of executives
- Board involved
- Extended leadership team
- External advisors

**What's been tried already?**
- Previous AI/ML initiatives and their outcomes
- Previous innovation lab attempts
- Digital transformation history
- What worked, what didn't, and why (their interpretation)

---

### 4.3 Deep Diagnostic Questions

These are the sophisticated questions that surface the real beliefs, constraints, and history. They assume an expert audience that has been thinking about these issues.

#### On Innovation Source Beliefs

**"When you think about the last three breakthroughs that really mattered for your company—the things that changed your trajectory—where did they actually come from? Internal R&D? An acquisition? A partnership? Someone in the field who figured something out? And is there a pattern?"**

*Why this question:* Reveals the organization's actual (not espoused) theory of where value comes from. The pattern matters more than any single example.

**"Your industry has a specific history with R&D and innovation. Pharma has the 10-year pipeline and the blockbuster model. Tech has fast iteration. Consumer goods has brand and distribution. How has that industry model shaped how you think about AI—and do you think AI changes the model itself, or operates within it?"**

*Why this question:* Surfaces whether they see AI as fitting into existing innovation patterns or disrupting them. Industry-specific framing shows sophistication.

**"Some of your peers are making big internal AI capability bets—Lilly's $1B NVIDIA lab, Novo's supercomputer. Others are going external—Servier's dual partnerships worth nearly $2B. What's your instinct on whether AI capability should be owned, accessed, or both? And does your answer differ by part of the value chain?"**

*Why this question:* Forces a position on build/buy/partner with specific peer examples. The "differ by part of value chain" addition tests for nuanced thinking.

#### On Organizational Physics

**"If I asked five of your direct reports where the most important tacit knowledge in your organization lives—the stuff that's hard to articulate but critical to how things actually work—would they give me the same answer? Where would they point?"**

*Why this question:* Surfaces where an AI lab might need to be located to access critical knowledge. Disagreement among reports reveals organizational complexity.

**"Walk me through a recent decision that required coordination across multiple parts of your organization. How did information flow? Where did it get stuck? What made it ultimately work or not work?"**

*Why this question:* Reveals actual information architecture, not the org chart version. Shows where handoffs fail.

**"You've been in this organization for [X years]. What's the most important thing that's changed about how decisions actually get made? And what's stubbornly stayed the same despite attempts to change it?"**

*Why this question:* Surfaces organizational dynamics and the "immune system" that might resist lab structures.

#### On AI Strategic Role

**"Let me push on something. When you say 'AI transformation,' what's the actual transformation? Is this about doing what you do now but faster/cheaper/better? Is it about doing fundamentally different things? Is it about changing who does what? How would you describe what success looks like in five years?"**

*Why this question:* Forces precision on what "transformation" actually means. Many executives use the word without having a clear picture.

**"If a competitor deployed AI in a way that genuinely threatened your position, what would that look like? Where are you actually vulnerable to AI-enabled disruption—and where is AI more about keeping up than pulling ahead?"**

*Why this question:* Surfaces the defensive vs. offensive stance on AI, and where the real strategic exposure is.

**"There's a debate in your industry about whether AI is a source of differentiation or table stakes. Where do you come down? And does your current investment level match that belief?"**

*Why this question:* Tests for alignment between stated belief and actual resource commitment. Misalignment reveals something important.

#### On Constraints and History

**"What would make the board say no to an AI initiative? Not the obvious stuff—I mean, what are the implicit constraints that shape what's even proposable?"**

*Why this question:* Surfaces the real decision space, which is often much smaller than it appears.

**"Most large organizations have tried some version of an innovation lab before—and most of those didn't deliver what was hoped. What's your version of that story? What was tried, why did it fail, and what did the organization learn—or fail to learn—from it?"**

*Why this question:* Reveals organizational antibodies and scar tissue that will affect any new lab structure.

**"If you gave a capable team a mandate and real resources to build AI capability, what would kill them? Not what would make them fail—what would actively kill them? Political dynamics, resource reallocation, integration challenges, talent attrition—where's the organizational threat?"**

*Why this question:* Identifies the organizational immune response that the lab design must survive.

**"Who in your organization is genuinely threatened by AI success—not because they're bad at their jobs, but because AI done well might make parts of what they do obsolete? How do you think about that?"**

*Why this question:* Surfaces the political economy of AI transformation, which often determines success more than technical capability.

#### On Urgency and Timeline

**"If nothing changes, what happens? Not the worst case—the realistic base case if you continue current trajectory."**

*Why this question:* Calibrates the actual burning platform, if any.

**"What would have to be true for you to consider this AI initiative a success in 18 months? In 3 years? Those are different questions—what's the difference?"**

*Why this question:* Forces distinction between early wins and transformation outcomes.

**"Some transformations are about survival, some are about positioning, some are about opportunism. Which is this? And does your organization agree?"**

*Why this question:* Surfaces whether there's shared understanding of urgency—misalignment here is a major risk factor.

---

### 4.4 Pattern Recognition and Synthesis

As the diagnostic progresses, the tool should be building a picture across six dimensions:

**Dimension 1: Innovation Source Belief**
*Where does this organization actually believe breakthrough value comes from?*
- Deep internal R&D (long cycles, proprietary science)
- External absorption (licensing, M&A, partnerships)
- Operational excellence (doing known things better)
- Customer/field proximity (insights from the front line)
- Recombination (connecting existing things in new ways)

**Dimension 2: AI Strategic Role**
*What role does AI play in their theory of competitive advantage?*
- Existential transformation (the business model changes)
- Major capability upgrade (same model, much better)
- Efficiency/productivity (cost reduction, speed)
- Table stakes (must have, not differentiating)
- Uncertain (still figuring it out)

**Dimension 3: Organizational Physics**
*How does work actually flow and where does power sit?*
- Highly modular (divisions operate independently)
- Integrated (cross-functional collaboration is core)
- Federated (some things central, some distributed)
- Where tacit knowledge lives (field, R&D, operations, headquarters)
- Actual power centers (business units, functions, geographies, individuals)

**Dimension 4: Constraint Profile**
*What are the binding constraints any design must accommodate?*
- Regulatory requirements
- Board/investor expectations
- Talent market realities
- Existing technology infrastructure
- Labor relations
- Geographic distribution
- Budget envelope and investment capacity

**Dimension 5: History and Antibodies**
*What has been tried and what happened?*
- Previous innovation lab attempts and outcomes
- Digital transformation history
- Organizational immune responses to past change
- Who got burned and carries scars
- What's been learned vs. what keeps being repeated

**Dimension 6: Urgency and Timeline**
*What's the transformation clock?*
- Existential (2 years or less, survival at stake)
- Strategic (3-5 years, competitive position at stake)
- Opportunistic (no burning platform, seeking advantage)
- Exploratory (trying to understand before committing)

## 5. The Generative Conversation

After the diagnostic has built a picture, the tool shifts to generative mode—helping the leadership team design a lab model that fits their context.

### 5.1 Surfacing Relevant Examples

Based on the diagnostic picture, the tool identifies which library examples are most relevant:

**"Given what you've shared about [specific insight from diagnostic], I want to point you to a few models that might resonate:**

**[Example 1]** - Here's why this is relevant to your situation: [specific connection to diagnostic findings]

**[Example 2]** - This one addresses [specific challenge or constraint they mentioned]

**[Example 3]** - This might be a counterexample—it's a different approach than I'd have guessed for you, but [reason to consider it]

**What's your reaction? What resonates and what feels off?"**

### 5.2 Applying Design Principles

For each principle that seems relevant, the tool explores how it might apply:

**"One of the principles that seems relevant to your situation is [Principle X]: [brief statement of the principle].**

**Here's how I see it applying: [specific connection to their context].**

**The test for whether you're actually doing this is: [the test question from the principle].**

**How would your organization perform on that test today? And what would have to change?"**

### 5.3 Surfacing Tensions

As a model takes shape, the tool identifies tensions with other principles in the architecture:

**"The direction we're heading—[summary of emerging lab model]—creates some tensions I want to name:**

**With your Measurement Philosophy:** If you're measuring [X], that creates pressure that might [specific tension with lab model].

**With your Ambidexterity Structure:** The lab model we're discussing requires [specific resource/attention], which tensions with [specific operational reality].

**With your Information Architecture:** This lab model assumes [specific information flow], but you described [contradictory reality].

**These aren't reasons not to proceed—they're tensions you'll need to manage. How do you want to think about them?"**

### 5.4 Building Toward the Artifact

As the conversation progresses, the tool is building toward the output artifact:

**For Section 2 (Model Choices):**
- Capturing which models resonated and why
- Capturing which models were rejected and why
- Documenting the emerging lab design

**For Section 3 (Tensions We're Accepting):**
- Naming the tensions surfaced
- Capturing how the team wants to manage them

**For Section 7 (Mandates by Role):**
- What this lab model implies for the CEO
- What it implies for P&L heads
- What it implies for whoever leads the lab
- What it implies for the crowd activation function
