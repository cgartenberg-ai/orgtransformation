## Cross-Cutting Patterns

### Pattern 1: AI Code Generation as Internal Transformation Signal
**Maturity:** Emerging
**Evidence:**
- **Google (Q4 2025):** ~50% of Google code now written by AI agents with human review (CFO Ashkenazi)
- **Microsoft (2026):** 30% of code AI-written (Nadella)

**Analysis:** These are not just deployment metrics — they signal that Big Tech is being transformed BY AI internally, not just building AI for others. This is the "AI-native organization" pattern applied to legacy tech companies. When half your code is AI-generated, the production function for software has fundamentally changed.

**Open Questions:**
- Does this apply to Google DeepMind specifically or just Google overall?
- What review/oversight structure exists for AI-generated code?
- What happens to the junior engineer career pipeline when AI writes the code?

### Pattern 2: Management Delayering Rhetoric Converges (Strengthens Existing Insight)
**Maturity:** Confirmed (strengthened)
**Existing Insight:** #management-delayering-convergent

**New Evidence from This Batch:**
- **Intel (Tan):** "Organizational complexity and bureaucratic processes have been slowly suffocating the culture of innovation" / "Bureaucracy kills innovation"
- **Meta (Wang memo):** "Fewer conversations will be required to make a decision, and each person will be more load-bearing"
- **Meta (Zuckerberg, Q4 2025):** "What many big teams at Meta do can now be accomplished by a single, very talented person"
- **Microsoft (Nadella):** Leaders must "work and act like Individual Contributors"

**Analysis:** The delayering rhetoric is now coming from CEOs across Big Tech with remarkable convergence. The economic logic is consistent: AI expands individual cognitive capacity, reducing the need for hierarchical information aggregation. The middle manager's role as information broker becomes redundant.

**Recommendation:** Update insight #management-delayering-convergent with Intel, Meta (Wang/Zuckerberg), and Microsoft evidence.

### Pattern 3: CEO/Executive Direct AI Leadership
**Maturity:** Emerging
**Evidence:**
- **Intel:** CEO Lip-Bu Tan directly leads AI after CTO Justin Hotard departed to OpenAI
- **Meta:** Alexandr Wang (28) as CAIO with direct Zuckerberg report
- **SK Telecom (prior batch):** CEO dual-hat over AI CIC
- **Nvidia (reference):** Huang's 60 direct reports, no middle layer

**Analysis:** CEOs are taking personal control of AI rather than delegating to traditional CTO/CIO structures. This suggests AI is too strategically critical to delegate — or that existing tech leadership pipelines don't produce AI-capable executives. The Intel case is particularly notable: when your CTO leaves for OpenAI, the CEO steps in directly rather than hiring a replacement.

**Potential New Insight:** "CEO as AI Leader" — consider whether this warrants standalone insight or is subsumed under existing mechanisms.

### Pattern 4: Research Autonomy vs. Product Urgency (Counter-Examples)
**Maturity:** Confirmed (strengthened)
**Existing Insight:** #meta-exploration-failure

**New Evidence:**

**Analysis:** This batch provides the most complete explore/execute failure case study in the collection:
1. Meta failed to protect research autonomy (Mechanism #1 failure)
2. Chief Scientist departed publicly
3. Founded new lab explicitly to pursue research agenda without product pressure
4. Anthropic demonstrates the alternative: structural protection within a product company

**Recommendation:** Update insight #meta-exploration-failure with AMI Labs founding as "natural experiment continuation" — we now have the full arc from failure to departure to new lab creation.

### Pattern 5: CapEx Arms Race at Unprecedented Scale
**Maturity:** Emerging
**Evidence:**
- **Meta:** $115-135B 2026 CapEx guidance (nearly doubled from $72.2B in 2025)
- **Google:** $175-185B 2026 CapEx guidance (nearly doubled from $91.4B in 2025)
- **Microsoft:** $37.5B quarterly CapEx (+77% YoY)
- **Amazon:** $200B+ CapEx (implied from AWS buildout)

**Analysis:** These are infrastructure-level commitments that lock in AI strategy for years. The combined 2026 CapEx guidance from Meta + Google + Microsoft + Amazon exceeds $500B — more than the GDP of many countries. This creates a structural barrier to entry and signals that these companies view AI infrastructure as existential.

**Theoretical Connection:** This is "commitment device" economics — massive sunk cost investments that make AI strategy irreversible and signal credibility to talent, customers, and competitors.

**Potential New Insight:** Consider "CapEx as Commitment Device" — the strategic use of infrastructure investment to lock in AI transformation.

### Pattern 6: Dual Model Strategy Emergence
**Maturity:** Emerging
**Evidence:**
- **Microsoft:** "We will have access to OpenAI models... And we'll build our own models with MAI. So we will always have a model level." (Nadella)
- **Meta:** External Scale AI partnership ($14.3B) + internal MSL model development
- **Anthropic:** Single model source (Claude) — counter-example

**Analysis:** Big Tech companies are hedging their model bets with dual strategies: external partnerships AND internal development. This reduces dependency risk but creates internal coordination challenges. Anthropic's single-source strategy is a deliberate counter-choice — they ARE the model company.
